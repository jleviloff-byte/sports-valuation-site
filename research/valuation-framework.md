# What's a Team Worth? — Valuation Framework Primer

**For internal use — "What's a Team Worth?" editorial research**
*Last updated: May 2026*

---

## Executive Summary

Professional sports franchises have undergone a structural re-rating over the past decade that no traditional DCF model would have predicted. From 2012 to 2024, the average NFL franchise appreciated ~650%, the average NBA franchise ~700%, and top-tier EPL clubs ~300% in USD terms. The drivers are not cyclical — they are structural: a media rights arms race, scarcity of supply against expanding pools of institutional capital, and the gradual transformation of franchises from sports businesses into vertically integrated real estate and entertainment platforms. This primer identifies the five primary valuation drivers, stress-tests the framework against COVID, traces the entry of institutional capital, evaluates the Forbes methodology, unpacks the stadium economics story, and benchmarks recent milestones.

---

## 1. The Five Primary Valuation Drivers

### 1.1 Media Rights — The Foundation

Media rights revenue is the single largest determinant of franchise value in North American sports, functioning as the equivalent of a long-duration bond for the entire league ecosystem. Rights deals set a floor under franchise valuations because they represent contracted, recurring, largely fixed cash flows that are distributed equally (or near-equally) among member clubs — meaning every franchise benefits even if it finishes last.

**Key data points:**
- The NFL's current media package (2023–2033) is worth approximately **$113 billion** across Amazon, ESPN/ABC, Fox, NBC, and CBS — roughly $10B+ per year distributed to 32 clubs, or ~$340M per team annually, before a dollar of ticket or sponsorship revenue is earned.
- The NBA's new media deal (beginning 2025–26) with Amazon, NBC, and ESPN is worth **~$76 billion** over 11 years, representing a ~160% increase over the prior $24B deal. The announcement alone caused analysts to revise NBA franchise values upward 40–60% within 12 months.
- The EPL's domestic broadcasting deal for 2022–25 was worth £5B (~$6.4B), with international rights adding another £5.1B. Total EPL rights now exceed £10B per cycle, up from under £1B in the early 1990s.

**Why this drives valuation:** Rights fees are distributed centrally, which means they compress intra-league risk. Even a poorly managed franchise captures its share. For buyers, this guaranteed floor makes sports franchises more like infrastructure or toll roads than operating businesses — hence the premium multiples.

**The streaming inflection:** The entry of Amazon (NFL Thursday Night), Apple (MLB Friday, MLS), and eventually Netflix (NFL Christmas games, WWE Raw) into live sports has introduced tech-sector balance sheets into the rights bidding process. Tech buyers value rights for subscriber acquisition and retention, not profitability per se — which creates a structurally higher ceiling on rights prices than traditional broadcast economics would support.

---

### 1.2 Market Size — The Multiplier

Market size amplifies every other driver. Large-market franchises command premium local media deals, higher sponsor rates, greater corporate suite demand, and — in the NBA especially — disproportionate national relevance. The relationship is nonlinear: going from a mid-tier to a top-5 market doesn't double value, it multiples it.

**Framework:**
- **Local media:** Pre-streaming, regional sports networks (RSNs) were enormous value multipliers. The Yankees' partial ownership of YES Network allowed them to capture both the rights fee paid and the network profit. RSN collapse (Sinclair's Diamond Sports bankruptcy in 2023) has paradoxically pushed teams toward direct-to-consumer streaming, potentially letting large-market clubs capture even more of the value chain.
- **Sponsor rates:** A Fortune 500 company buying a jersey patch on the Golden State Warriors or the Dallas Cowboys pays a material premium to the same patch on a small-market team. The top 5 NBA jersey patch deals (Warriors, Lakers, Nets, Knicks, Bulls) averaged ~$30M/year vs. a league median closer to $8M.
- **Corporate suite demand:** Madison Square Garden's luxury suite waitlist is measured in decades. Lucas Oil Stadium's is measured in years. This gap compounds at renewal — MSG can push aggressive rate increases; smaller markets cannot.

**International relevance as a new axis:** The NBA's global following (particularly in China, Africa, and Europe) has created a secondary market-size driver: international fan base. LeBron James-era Lakers and Curry-era Warriors were worth materially more because of international market penetration. The NFL is attempting to replicate this via London and Frankfurt games.

---

### 1.3 Brand / IP — The Intangible Premium

Brand is the residual after you control for market size and media rights — it's what separates the Cowboys ($9B+) from the Commanders ($6B) despite being in similar-sized markets, or Manchester United from Everton despite both being in England.

**Brand components in sports:**
1. **Legacy and trophies:** Sustained winning creates durable fan bases that persist through bad seasons. The Celtics' 18 championships, the Patriots' dynasty, Barcelona's global brand — these survive coaching changes and roster turnover.
2. **Superstar association:** LeBron's Lakers affiliation, Jordan's Bulls, and now Mahomes' Chiefs created generational brand equity that compresses with the star's departure but never fully reverts. The halo effect on merchandise, media, and sponsorship is measurable and durable.
3. **Cultural relevance:** The Cowboys brand was built by Jerry Jones' media strategy and a late-80s/early-90s dynasty, but it has sustained primetime placement, cultural cameos, and sponsor premiums for 30 years. The Dallas market alone cannot explain why the Cowboys are worth nearly 50% more than the next most valuable NFL team.
4. **Merchandise / licensing:** NFL and NBA IP generates billions annually from licensing. Top brands command premium royalty rates and more favorable terms from licensees.

**Quantifying the brand premium:** Forbes attempts to isolate brand via a "Market Value" methodology, but the cleanest signal is the spread between transaction price and Forbes pre-transaction estimate — top brands consistently transact at 20–40% premiums to Forbes.

### 1.4 Stadium Ownership / Real Estate — The New Value Frontier

Stadium ownership (or favorable stadium economics) has become the single fastest-growing value driver in sports, effectively transforming franchise buyers from sports operators into real estate developers with captive anchor tenants.

See Section 5 for full treatment.

### 1.5 On-Field Performance — The Weakest Driver

Counterintuitively, sustained on-field performance has the *weakest* long-term impact on valuations relative to the other four drivers. Short-term revenue effects are real (playoff gates, TV bonuses, sponsor activation), but they don't compound into structural value the way media rights do.

**Why winning matters less than you think:**
- Revenue sharing in the NFL is so aggressive that a Super Bowl appearance adds perhaps $30–50M in incremental revenue — material but not transformative relative to a $5–8B asset.
- The Yankees haven't won the World Series since 2009 but remain the most valuable MLB franchise.
- The Knicks haven't won an NBA title since 1973 and are consistently among the top 3 most valuable NBA franchises.
- The Detroit Lions, despite decades of futility, increased in value substantially — driven by the media rights floor and Detroit market dynamics, not performance.

**Where it matters:** Winning matters most in leagues with less revenue sharing (EPL) and for franchises that are early in their brand-building arc. A newly relocated or expansion franchise with weak performance can damage long-term fan development. For established franchises in major markets, the floor is set by structural factors, not the scoreboard.

## 2. COVID as a Demand Inelasticity Proof-of-Concept

### The Natural Experiment Nobody Planned

COVID-19 was the first exogenous stress test of the modern sports franchise valuation model. For 12–18 months, sports leagues operated with zero (or severely reduced) in-person attendance — eliminating gate revenue, concessions, parking, and ancillary streams that historically represented 30–50% of team revenue. The playbook for valuation skeptics predicted a sharp correction in franchise values. The opposite occurred.

### What Actually Happened

**Valuations held and then accelerated:**
- The NFL sold the Denver Broncos in August 2022 for **$4.65 billion** — a then-record for any sports franchise globally — despite two prior years of reduced attendance revenue. The Walmart heirs' Walton-Penner group paid a price that implied they expected accelerating media rights deals to more than offset any COVID hangover.
- The NBA sold the Phoenix Suns in December 2022 for **$4 billion**, again a then-record for the NBA, and again transacting *after* COVID had fully played out.
- Forbes' aggregate NFL franchise values increased ~35% from 2020 to 2023 — essentially uninterrupted by the pandemic.

### The Structural Explanation

COVID proved three things that valuation models had always assumed but never tested:

1. **Broadcast revenue is untethered from attendance.** The NFL, NBA, and NCAA negotiated broadcast deals as if games would be played — and they were. Media partners (who have their own contractual distribution obligations to advertisers) had almost no leverage to renegotiate despite empty stadiums. Games with zero fans generated the same national rights fees.

2. **Fan demand is inelastic at the brand level.** Ratings did not collapse. Viewership held or increased in several leagues (NFL ratings were up 10% in 2020 vs. 2019). The scarcity of live sports content during shutdown months created pent-up demand that translated directly into the post-COVID stadium attendance surge of 2021–2023, where many leagues posted record attendance numbers.

3. **The asset is the rights, not the event.** The physical game is a content delivery mechanism for media rights, which are the real asset. Once that frame is internalized, COVID didn't damage the asset — it temporarily impaired one delivery channel while the underlying contractual value remained intact.

### COVID as Accelerant for Valuations

Paradoxically, COVID *accelerated* the institutional investor thesis for sports franchises. The demonstration that values held through a near-worst-case scenario validated sports as an alternative asset class. PE funds and sovereign wealth vehicles that had been circling sports investments treated the COVID stress test as the final proof point they needed.

## 3. Institutional Capital: The Structural Demand Shock

### 3.1 Why Institutions Arrived Late

Until the 2010s, major North American sports leagues had ownership rules that effectively excluded institutional capital:

- **NFL:** Required majority ownership by a single individual or family; prohibited public companies, pension funds, and private equity.
- **NBA:** Similar structure; corporations could hold minority stakes but operating control had to be human.
- **MLB:** Same framework; the Montreal Expos going to a trust owned by other owners was an anomaly, not a model.

These rules reflected a founders-era belief that individual owners with "skin in the game" managed teams better than institutions. The rules also, not incidentally, suppressed valuations by limiting the buyer pool.

### 3.2 The Policy Shift

The NBA moved first in 2021, approving private equity ownership (up to 20% non-controlling stakes) from a small list of approved funds. The NFL voted in 2023 to allow institutional investor ownership for the first time — approved funds can take up to 10% non-controlling stakes, with a total institutional cap of 30% per team.

The approved funds are now a who's-who of alternative asset managers: Ares Management, Arctos Sports Partners, Dyal Homecourt (Blue Owl), Sixth Street, and others. These aren't passive vehicles — they are dedicated sports-focused funds with GP teams that have deep league relationships and domain expertise.

### 3.3 Sovereign Wealth Enters the Field

Sovereign wealth funds (SWFs) have pursued sports investments as part of broader "sportswashing" and economic diversification mandates:

- **Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund (PIF):** Acquired Newcastle United FC (2021) for ~$400M; bankrolled LIV Golf as a direct competitor to the PGA Tour; discussed potential NFL franchise interests. PIF manages ~$700B in assets.
- **Abu Dhabi's Mubadala / Sheikh Mansour:** City Football Group (Manchester City's parent) is majority Abu Dhabi-owned; CFG has expanded to 13 clubs across 6 continents, creating a multi-club portfolio model.
- **Qatar Sports Investments (QSI):** Owns Paris Saint-Germain; used the club as anchor for the 2022 World Cup soft power campaign.

**Why SWFs pay what others won't:** SWFs are not optimizing for IRR in the traditional sense. They are optimizing for reputational rebranding, geopolitical soft power, and economic diversification mandates from their home governments. This creates a structurally higher ceiling for assets that carry cultural prestige — exactly what marquee sports franchises offer.

### 3.4 Multi-Club Ownership: The PE Portfolio Model

The City Football Group playbook — own multiple clubs across leagues and geographies, share coaching infrastructure, scouting databases, and data analytics — has become the dominant institutional investment thesis in soccer. It treats football clubs as a roll-up opportunity with operational synergies.

- RedBird Capital (owns AC Milan, partial Fenway Sports Group, Liverpool)
- Silver Lake (owns 10% of Manchester City/CFG, 10% of New Zealand All Blacks)
- 777 Partners (owned Genoa, Standard Liège, Hertha BSC before financial collapse — a cautionary tale on leverage)
- Clearlake Capital (owns Chelsea)

The multi-club model is still evolving in North American sports due to cross-ownership prohibitions, but leagues are under pressure to revisit these rules as institutional buyers demand portfolio construction flexibility.

### 3.5 The ZIRP Legacy and Rate Environment Headwinds

Much of the 2015–2022 sports valuation surge coincided with near-zero interest rates, which compressed discount rates across all asset classes and pushed capital toward alternatives. Rising rates (2022–2024) created modest valuation headwinds — but sports franchises demonstrated relative resilience because the *fundamental* driver (media rights escalation) continued regardless of rate environment. The key question for 2026–2030 is whether new NBA and NFL media deals can continue to escalate faster than the cost of capital — if yes, the multiple expansion story continues; if not, valuations plateau even as absolute earnings grow.

## 4. The Forbes Methodology: Signal, Noise, and What's Missing

### 4.1 What Forbes Does

Forbes has published annual sports franchise valuation estimates since 1998. The methodology is a blend of:

- **Revenue-based approach:** Forbes estimates revenue from public sources (league-disclosed sharing data, stadium attendance figures, sponsorship disclosures, media contracts) and applies an estimated EBITDA margin. This produces an earnings estimate.
- **Comparable transactions:** Recent sales inform the multiple applied to those earnings. Forbes uses a blend of sports-specific and entertainment-sector comparables.
- **Asset adjustments:** Stadium ownership and real estate are partially captured via adjustments to the base earnings multiple.
- **Brand premium:** Somewhat subjective adjustments for market size, brand heritage, and cultural relevance.

Forbes does not have access to franchise financial statements — it is estimating from outside. The methodology is directionally correct but precision-limited.

### 4.2 What Forbes Gets Right

- **Ranking stability:** Forbes ordinal rankings (who is most to least valuable) are reliable and consistent. The Cowboys being #1 in the NFL for a decade tracks real market signals.
- **Trend direction:** Forbes aggregate appreciation curves match transaction data well over 5–10 year horizons.
- **Accessibility:** Forbes valuations have democratized the conversation about sports as an asset class. Their annual release generates meaningful media coverage that educates fans.

### 4.3 What Forbes Misses or Undervalues

**Real estate / development upside:** Forbes struggles to capture the optionality value embedded in stadium district development rights. A franchise that owns 50 acres around its stadium in a gentrifying urban core has a real estate development company attached to it — and that isn't in Forbes' revenue-multiple model.

**Non-operating asset value:** Content libraries, documentary rights (NBA on Netflix, Formula 1's *Drive to Survive* model), social media IP, gaming/esports adjacencies, and data licensing are emergent revenue streams that aren't yet in Forbes' revenue base.

**International revenue potential:** Forbes is U.S.-revenue-centric. NBA and NFL international revenue is still early-stage relative to what EPL clubs generate from Asia and Middle East markets. As the NBA Global deal develops, Forbes will chronically undercount international revenue potential for top-brand franchises.

**Structural scarcity premium:** Forbes values teams as going-concern businesses, not as scarce alternatives. There are 32 NFL teams and there will never be more than 32 NFL teams (expansion is theoretically possible but politically difficult). The scarcity value of membership in a protected cartel isn't captured in EBITDA multiples — it's a separate premium that only reveals itself in auction processes.

**Control premium / transaction premia:** Forbes estimates are typically 20–40% *below* actual transaction prices for marquee assets. The 2023 Washington Commanders sale ($6.05B) exceeded Forbes' prior-year estimate by ~30%. The 2019 Brooklyn Nets/Barclays Center sale implied a 40%+ premium to Forbes. Systematic underestimation is a feature of the model, not a flaw — Forbes is estimating fair value, not auction clearing prices, which include control premiums, synergies, and strategic buyer logic.

**The "hobby money" distortion:** Ultra-high-net-worth buyers (tech billionaires, sports-obsessed wealth) will pay non-economic prices for legacy franchises. The Walton family buying the Broncos at $4.65B, or Steve Cohen buying the Mets at $2.4B, reflects personal utility and trophy value that no financial model captures.

## 5. Stadium Economics and Real Estate as a Value Driver

### 5.1 The Old Model: Tenant Franchises

For most of the 20th century, franchises were *tenants* — they played in publicly owned stadiums built with municipal bonds and operated by city agencies, paying below-market rents in exchange for economic development justifications (disputed in the academic literature but politically persistent).

This model was value-neutral for franchise owners: they avoided capital risk but captured none of the stadium upside.

### 5.2 The New Model: Franchise-as-Developer

The transformative insight of the past 20 years is that a sports franchise — particularly in the NFL and NBA — is the most powerful anchor tenant imaginable. It generates predictable, date-certain foot traffic 8–12 times per year (NFL games) or 40+ times per year (NBA). That predictability is the foundation of commercial real estate underwriting.

Franchise owners who recognized this began acquiring not just stadiums but the surrounding land, turning "sports districts" into vertically integrated mixed-use developments:

**Case studies:**

- **SoFi Stadium (Los Angeles Rams/Chargers):** Stan Kroenke privately financed a $5.5B stadium (the most expensive in history) plus 298 surrounding acres in Inglewood — now being developed as a mixed-use entertainment, retail, and hospitality district. The stadium is the anchor; the real estate is the business.

- **Golden 1 Center / Sacramento Kings:** The Kings' arena is anchored in a downtown Sacramento development district that includes hotels, office space, and residential. The arena made the district viable; the district generates returns beyond basketball.

- **Oakland A's — Las Vegas Stadium:** The A's leveraged their relocation to secure a stadium deal that includes significant surrounding land rights near the Las Vegas Strip. The real estate speculation embedded in the relocation calculus is not incidental — it's the thesis.

- **NFL Stadium Pipeline (2024–2030):** Bills (Buffalo), Bears (Chicago lakefront), 49ers (existing Santa Clara deal), Raiders (Las Vegas, operational). Each of these deals includes some form of surrounding development rights. The Bills stadium deal (~$1.4B in public subsidy) reflects how much leverage an NFL franchise has as an anchor tenant — municipalities compete aggressively for the economic activity.

- **Cotai Strip parallel:** Vegas sports deals are explicitly following the casino-hotel-arena bundled model. MGM/Allegiant area development around Raiders stadium mirrors the entertainment district logic that built the Vegas Strip.

### 5.3 The Cap Rate Arbitrage

The insight that drives the stadium-as-real-estate thesis:

- Arena/stadium real estate trades at **cap rates of 4–6%** (similar to premium retail or hospitality) when underwritten with a creditworthy anchor tenant on a long-term lease.
- Sports franchise operating businesses trade at **EBITDA multiples of 20–30x** — equivalent cap rates of ~3–5%.
- If you own both the franchise (anchor tenant) AND the real estate, you are effectively cross-subsidizing the real estate investment with the sporting business. The franchise guarantees the foot traffic; the real estate captures the value.

This arbitrage is explicit in how Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, and dedicated sports finance groups now pitch the sports investment thesis to institutional clients.

### 5.4 The Public Subsidy Distortion

Academic economists (Noll, Zimbalist, Rosentraub) have consistently found that public stadium subsidies don't deliver their promised economic returns to municipalities. But the political economy of professional sports makes subsidy extraction robust:

- Credible relocation threats (Raiders to Vegas, Rams to LA, SuperSonics to Oklahoma City) have demonstrated that leagues will approve moves, giving franchise owners genuine leverage.
- Local identity and civic pride make opposition to stadium subsidies politically costly even when economics argue against them.
- The asymmetry: franchise owners capture private returns on public investment; municipalities capture diffuse civic benefits that are hard to quantify and politically perishable.

For franchise valuation purposes, the practical effect is that owners frequently receive below-market financing, favorable land leases, infrastructure contributions (roads, transit connections), and tax abatements — all of which are capitalized into franchise value but don't appear in franchise P&L.

## 6. Valuation Milestones and League Trajectories

### 6.1 The $10 Billion Franchise: Dallas Cowboys

The Cowboys crossed $10B in Forbes' valuation in the 2023 estimates, making them the first sports franchise in any league to reach that milestone. The figure is more symbolic than operational — it reflects the confluence of:

- The NFL's media rights superiority (the $10B/year deal dwarfs all other U.S. leagues)
- Jerry Jones' brand-building over 35 years (Cowboys as "America's Team")
- AT&T Stadium's capacity and suite economics
- The Cowboys' disproportionate national primetime allocation

The $10B headline created a benchmark effect: every other NFL franchise was implicitly revalued upward against it, and competing leagues (NBA, MLB, EPL) felt pressure to articulate how their flagship franchises approach comparable figures.

### 6.2 NFL Trajectory

The NFL is the most valuable sports league in the world by aggregate franchise value. Key milestones:

| Year | Record Sale Price | Franchise | Implied League Avg. |
|------|------------------|-----------|---------------------|
| 2010 | ~$1.0B | Various | ~$1.0B |
| 2018 | $2.35B | Carolina Panthers | ~$2.5B |
| 2019 | $3.05B | Denver Broncos (partial) | ~$3.0B |
| 2022 | $4.65B | Denver Broncos (full) | ~$4.5B |
| 2023 | $6.05B | Washington Commanders | ~$5.5B |
| 2024 | $6.35B | Miami Dolphins (partial at implied val.) | ~$6.0B |

**2026 outlook:** With the media rights floor at ~$340M/team/year and expansion to 18 regular season games under discussion, the average NFL franchise value likely clears $6–7B by end of decade. A marquee franchise (Cowboys, Giants, Patriots in a favorable transaction) could test $12–15B by 2030.

### 6.3 NBA Trajectory

The NBA has historically traded at a discount to the NFL given smaller market sizes, less revenue sharing, and an older media deal. The 2025 media deal restructuring — worth ~$76B over 11 years vs. $24B previously — is a categorical reset:

| Year | Record Sale Price | Franchise |
|------|------------------|-----------|
| 2014 | $2.0B | Los Angeles Clippers |
| 2019 | $2.35B | Brooklyn Nets (partial) |
| 2022 | $4.0B | Phoenix Suns |
| 2023 | $3.5B | Utah Jazz (partial, lower implied) |
| 2024 | ~$6.0B | Boston Celtics |

The Celtics sale at ~$6B (2024) was the definitive signal that the NBA new media deal had been priced in by buyers. At $6B for a team in a legacy market with a storied brand and 18 championships, the NBA has essentially closed the gap with mid-tier NFL franchises. Marquee NBA assets (Lakers, Knicks, Warriors) likely clear $8–10B in any near-term transaction.

### 6.4 EPL Trajectory

EPL club valuations operate on different mechanics — promotion/relegation risk, Champions League qualification dependence, and a much longer history of institutional ownership create a different risk/return profile:

| Club | Approx. 2024 Valuation | Owner |
|------|------------------------|-------|
| Manchester United | ~$6.5B | INEOS (Ratcliffe, 25% stake at £1.25B implied valuation; full control discussion ongoing) |
| Manchester City | ~$6.0B | Abu Dhabi United Group |
| Liverpool | ~$5.5B | Fenway Sports Group |
| Arsenal | ~$4.0B | Stan Kroenke |
| Chelsea | ~$3.5B | Clearlake Capital |
| Tottenham | ~$3.0B | ENIC Group |

**EPL vs. NFL comparison:** The top EPL clubs are approaching NFL mid-tier valuations but face structural headwinds the NFL doesn't: they are subject to UEFA financial regulations (FFP/Sustainability Rules), Champions League qualification risk (missing CL can cost £100M+ in annual revenue), and promotion/relegation risk at the bottom. These risks compress EPL multiples relative to NFL, which has no relegation, guaranteed media sharing, and a more favorable competitive balance structure.

**The global brand premium offset:** However, Manchester United and Liverpool have genuinely global fan bases of 600M+ and 500M+ respectively (admittedly soft numbers from club surveys), giving them international commercial revenue that no NFL team has yet achieved at scale. As the NFL globalizes, the gap may narrow.

### 6.5 MLB and NHL: Divergent Stories

**MLB:** Valuations have grown but more slowly than NFL/NBA. The Yankees (~$7B+) are an outlier driven by YES Network ownership and historic brand. The Yankees crossed $7B in Forbes' 2023 estimates. Most MLB franchises trade at $1–3B with wide dispersion based on market size. The next CBA and domestic media deal (post-RSN collapse) will be the key catalyst.

**NHL:** The most undervalued North American league by franchise price per revenue dollar. The Seattle Kraken's expansion fee ($650M in 2018) implied a league-wide valuation basis, but top franchises (Original Six especially — Canadiens, Leafs, Rangers) trade well above. The NHL's media deal is materially smaller than NFL/NBA, capping the rights floor. International hockey growth (particularly in developing markets) is slower than basketball globalization, limiting upside.

## 7. Synthesis: The Valuation Matrix

The following framework weights primary value drivers across leagues:

| Driver | NFL Weight | NBA Weight | EPL Weight | MLB Weight |
|--------|-----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Media Rights | 40% | 35% | 25% | 30% |
| Market Size | 20% | 25% | 20% | 25% |
| Brand / IP | 20% | 20% | 30% | 20% |
| Stadium / Real Estate | 15% | 15% | 15% | 20% |
| On-Field Performance | 5% | 5% | 10% | 5% |

**Key takeaways:**
1. Media rights dominate in all leagues, but especially the NFL where centralized sharing is most aggressive.
2. Brand/IP outweighs market size in the EPL because global fan bases are more dispersed than U.S. markets.
3. On-field performance barely registers in the long-run valuation calculus for NFL/NBA/MLB; it matters more in the EPL due to promotion/relegation and Champions League stakes.
4. Stadium/real estate is the fastest-growing driver and is likely to increase in weight over the 2025–2035 period as more franchise owners execute the developer thesis.

## 8. Key Risks to the Valuation Thesis

Not a bull-only story. Material risks:

- **Cord-cutting / streaming economics:** If streaming sports rights are priced for subscriber acquisition but streaming services can't monetize subscribers efficiently, media rights escalation slows or reverses. The RSN collapse (Sinclair's Diamond Sports filing) is a preview of this risk at the local level.
- **Antitrust / league structure:** U.S. sports leagues benefit from antitrust exemptions (MLB has an explicit one; NFL has a narrower one for broadcasting). Regulatory changes or judicial challenges could disrupt the cartel economics that underpin valuations.
- **Salary cap / labor dynamics:** Player empowerment (NBA in particular) and periodic labor disputes can disrupt revenues. A prolonged work stoppage is the operational risk scenario.
- **Interest rate environment:** At sustained high rates, leveraged buyers are disadvantaged, which compresses the pool of potential acquirers and can slow transaction-based price discovery.
- **Social license / viewership demographics:** NFL viewership is aging. NBA ratings have been more volatile. If the next generation of sports fans fragments across gaming, esports, and social content rather than concentrating on live sports, the long-term rights escalation thesis weakens.
- **International overreach:** Multi-club ownership and global expansion strategies introduce regulatory, FX, and governance complexity. The 777 Partners collapse is a cautionary tale on leverage and complexity in cross-border sports investment.
